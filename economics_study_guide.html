CHAPTER D2

Market Failure & Government Intervention

In a perfect free market, supply and demand allocate resources efficiently. But the real world isn't perfect. When the market produces too much, too little, or none of a good compared to what society actually needs, we call this market failure. When this happens, governments step in to fix it.

I. What does "market failure" actually mean?

An efficient market allocates resources optimally — every resource goes where it creates the most value. A market fails when this doesn't happen. There are three forms of failure:

  • Too much of a good (e.g. cigarettes — society would prefer less)
  • Too little of a good (e.g. healthcare for the poor)
  • None of the good (e.g. street lighting — no private firm makes it)
Why this matters for the exam: Whenever you see "why should the government intervene?" — the answer always starts with: because the market has failed in one of six ways. Learn the six causes and you can answer almost any question on this chapter.

II. The six types of market failure

Click each one to expand the full explanation.

1. Lack of competition

A perfectly competitive market has many buyers AND many sellers. When this isn't the case, prices rise, quantities fall, and quality suffers.

Few sellersFew buyers
Monopoly = 1 seller (e.g. Microsoft, SNCB)Monopsony = 1 buyer
Duopoly = 2 sellersDuopsony = 2 buyers
Oligopoly = a few sellers (e.g. Orange, SFR, Bouygues, Free)Oligopsony = a few buyers

When competition is lacking, sellers can:

  • Charge prices that are too high
  • Produce quantities that are too low
  • Offer poor quality (bad service, no innovation)
The EU has fined many large tech firms for anti-competitive behaviour: Apple (€1.8B in 2024), Google (€4.34B in 2018). The EU Competition department scrutinises markets and can block mergers. The textbook lists the SEC as the US equivalent — use that on the exam.
2. Missing markets — public & merit goods

Some goods are not provided AT ALL by the private market (public goods), or only in TOO SMALL quantities (merit goods).

Public goods — two defining features

  • Non-rivalry (non-diminishability): one person using it doesn't reduce what's available to others. The lighthouse beam doesn't dim because more ships see it.
  • Non-excludability: you can't stop people from using it. You can't block someone from seeing the lighthouse light.
The free rider problem:
→ If one person pays, everyone gets it for free
→ So no one wants to pay
→ So no private firm can make a profit
→ So the market produces NONE of it

Solution: Government provides it and pays through taxes.

Classifying any good — the 2×2 matrix

Use this matrix to classify ANY good. Two questions: is it diminishable (rival)? Is it excludable?

Merit goods

Different from public goods. They CAN be provided privately, but they would be UNDER-provided — too few people could afford them.

Society as a whole benefits when more people consume them (positive externalities), so government steps in to make sure everyone has access.

Examples: vaccination, education, healthcare, museums, sport facilities.

3. Demerit goods

The opposite of merit goods. Socially undesirable — they harm both the consumer and society. The free market OVER-provides them because consumers don't fully realise (or don't care) how harmful they are.

Examples: drugs, alcohol, cigarettes, child pornography, guns.

Government reduces production/consumption through taxes, bans, age limits, and warning campaigns.

4. Inequalities

The free market produces large income and wealth differences. According to the World Inequality Lab data cited in your notes (2016), the top 10% earned around 37% of national income in Europe versus around 61% in the Middle East.

Governments correct this through:

  • Progressive tax system — higher tax rates on higher incomes
  • Social aid / benefits — unemployment benefits, health insurance, minimum living allowances
5. Imperfect information

In a perfect market, buyers and sellers share the same information. In reality, one party (usually the seller) knows more. This is called asymmetric information.

  • The seller of a second-hand house knows about the leaking roof; the buyer doesn't
  • An insurance customer knows their health risks; the insurer doesn't

This leads to bad decisions — the market doesn't reach optimal equilibrium.

6. Externalities (the most important one)

Read this carefully — externalities come up in almost every exam.

Core definition: An externality is when the production or consumption of a good affects a THIRD PARTY — someone who is neither the buyer nor the seller.

If the third party is harmed → negative externality.
If the third party benefits → positive externality.

Private cost vs Social cost

  • Private cost: cost to the producer/consumer doing the activity (e.g. for a firm: TC = FC + VC)
  • Social cost: cost to the whole society, including third parties
Social cost = Private cost + External cost
Social benefit = Private benefit + External benefit
Memorise both — they appear in essay questions constantly

Negative externalities (over-produced)

Two flavours:

  • Production externality: harm from MAKING the good. A BMW factory pollutes, creates noise, traffic jams.
  • Consumption externality: harm from USING the good. Driving a BMW causes pollution, accidents.

Quick rule: ALL demerit goods produce negative externalities.

Positive externalities (under-produced)

  • Production externality: training employees, language courses, R&D research
  • Consumption externality: electric cars, vaccinations, renovating your house façade

Quick rule: ALL merit goods produce positive externalities.

A good can produce BOTH positive and negative externalities. An electric car has positives (less pollution) but also negatives (battery production pollutes; accidents still happen). Trees in your garden look nice for the neighbour (positive) but might drop leaves on their car (negative).

CHAPTER D3

Labour Markets

The labour market is just like any other market — there's a SUPPLY of workers and a DEMAND for workers. The price is the WAGE RATE. If the market were perfect, supply and demand would meet at the equilibrium wage. But labour markets are NEVER perfect — and when supply > demand, you get UNEMPLOYMENT.

I. Supply of labour

The supply of labour = all the people willing AND able to work. Also called the labour force, the working population, or the active population.

Who counts — and who doesn't

INCLUDED (the economically active):

  • Employed
  • Self-employed
  • Civil servants
  • Armed forces
  • Unemployed (looking for a job)

EXCLUDED (economically inactive):

  • Very young people / children
  • Students
  • Stay-at-home parents
  • Retired / elderly
  • Those unable to work
Unemployed people ARE part of the labour supply (they are willing and able). Don't confuse 'unemployed' with 'inactive'.
Why does labour supply change?
  • Working conditions: fringe benefits, holidays, atmosphere, promotion prospects
  • Demographic changes: migration, retirement age, birth rate
  • Tax system: high tax discourages work
  • Legislation and women's role
  • Education and training quality
Why do wages differ between jobs?
  • Different skills/qualifications: doctors earn more than nurses
  • Different responsibilities: multinational CEO vs small-firm director
  • "Dirty" jobs and unsocial hours get pay bonuses
  • Profit margins: finance pays more than retail
  • Job satisfaction: intrinsically rewarding jobs (nursing, teaching) often pay less
  • Labour immobility (geographical and occupational)
  • Lack of information about available jobs
Why do people doing the same job earn differently?
  • Expatriation premium
  • Discrimination (gender, age, ethnicity, etc.)
  • Fringe benefits
  • Union-negotiated local pay agreements
  • Length of service / seniority
  • Previous experience
  • Location (large cities pay more)

II. Demand for labour

Demand for labour = all the people and firms willing AND able to employ workers.

Two key features you MUST remember:
  • Derived demand — firms don't want labour for its own sake. They want labour because workers produce goods customers will buy. No demand for typewriters → no demand for typewriter-makers.
  • Downward-sloping in wages — if wages rise, firms hire fewer workers; if wages fall, firms hire more.
Why does demand for labour change?
  • Consumer demand changes for the firm's product (typists, coal miners — declined)
  • Worker productivity changes — more productive = more demand
  • Cost/productivity of capital — cheaper machines mean firms substitute machines for workers
  • Non-wage employment costs — higher fringe benefits or health insurance → less demand for labour

III. Wage determination & unemployment

In a perfect labour market, supply and demand of labour meet at the equilibrium wage. But this rarely happens. When the wage is ABOVE equilibrium, supply > demand — and the excess supply is unemployment.

Unemployment: people of working age, willing and able to work, without a paid job, but ACTIVELY LOOKING for employment. (Someone who has given up looking is "inactive", not unemployed.)

The two rates you must know

Unemployment rate = (Unemployed ÷ Labour force) × 100
Labour force = working age population − economically inactive
Employment rate = (Employed ÷ Working age population) × 100
Note: this uses a different denominator from the unemployment rate
Employment rate is NOT equal to 100% − unemployment rate. They use different denominators. The EU target for the employment rate is 78%.
Worked example — calculate the unemployment rate

Given:

  • Working age population = 150,000
  • Employed = 120,000
  • Economically inactive = 20,000

Step 1: Labour force = 150,000 − 20,000 = 130,000

Step 2: Unemployed = 130,000 − 120,000 = 10,000

Step 3: Unemployment rate = (10,000 ÷ 130,000) × 100 = 7.69%

8 reasons real labour markets are imperfect
  • a) Minimum wage legislation — above market wage = less demand
  • b) Trade unions — push wages up without productivity gains
  • c) Unemployment benefits — may reduce work incentive (voluntary unemployment)
  • d) Lack of information — workers don't know jobs exist
  • e) Existing contracts — wages can't fall mid-contract
  • f) Labour immobility — geographical (can't move) or occupational (lack skills)
  • g) Discrimination — by ethnicity, gender, age
  • h) Monopsony — single buyer can force wages down

IV. Rigid vs flexible labour markets

RIGID (France, Belgium)FLEXIBLE (UK, US)
Many rules and regulationsFew regulations
Strong minimum wageOften no minimum, or low
Powerful trade unionsWeaker unions; market sets wages
Hard to fire workersEasy to hire and fire
Less short-term variation in unemploymentUnemployment moves fast with the economy
Long-term unemployment tends to be HIGHERLong-term unemployment tends to be LOWER
During the global recession, the US and France had similar GDP drops, but US unemployment jumped ~5 percentage points; France only ~1.5. Over 25 years however, the US average is around 5% versus France around 9.6%. This is the rigid-vs-flexible trade-off.
Advantages of flexible labour markets
  • Firms stay competitive — they can adjust workforce, avoid bankruptcy
  • Higher productivity → more output, more exports, lower inflation
  • More choice for workers who want flexible hours (e.g. more female participation)
  • Attracts foreign investment (multinationals like flexibility)
  • Lower structural unemployment
Disadvantages of flexible labour markets
  • Part-time / temporary workers get less training
  • Job insecurity → lower morale, lower productivity
  • Rising inequality between "insiders" and "outsiders"
  • Risk of low-paid "working poor"
  • Smaller pensions for short-contract workers

Flexicurity — the Danish model

Denmark combines the best of flexibility AND security. The system rests on THREE pillars — the Golden Triangle:

The Golden Triangle:

1. Flexibility — Easy to hire and fire. Your notes say about 25% of Danish private sector workers change jobs each year (this figure is widely cited but worth confirming for any specific exam reference).

2. Social security — Generous unemployment benefits for up to two years (this is the figure in your notes; the actual current Danish duration has been reduced over recent years — for the exam, use what your textbook says).

3. Active labour market policies — Heavy state investment in education, retraining, and job-seeking help.

Results: high job mobility, trust-based social dialogue, lower long-term unemployment.

CHAPTER E

Principles of Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics looks at the economy AS A WHOLE. Money flows IN (injections) and OUT (withdrawals). When in = out, the economy is in equilibrium. When out, the economy either grows (with inflation) or shrinks (with unemployment).

E1. The circular flow of income

Four agents trade money with each other:

  • Households — you and your family
  • Businesses — firms producing goods
  • Government — public sector
  • World economy — other countries
The 3 leakages (money leaving) and 3 injections (money entering)

LEAKAGES (withdrawals):

  • T = Taxes — money goes to government
  • S = Savings — money goes to bank instead of being spent
  • M = Imports — money leaves the country

INJECTIONS:

  • G = Government spending — infrastructure, schools, etc.
  • I = Investment — firms buying new equipment
  • X = Exports — foreigners buying our goods
G + I + X = T + S + M
Equilibrium: injections equal leakages

Think of the economy as a bathtub:

  • In = Out: water level stays the same → equilibrium
  • In > Out: level rises → growth, but risk of overheating & inflation
  • In < Out: level falls → recession, unemployment risk

E2. Measuring national income — GDP

GDP (Gross Domestic Product) = the market value of all FINAL goods and services produced WITHIN a country during a given period (usually a year).
GDP vs GNP — the Audi example
  • GDP = where the production happens (within the country's borders)
  • GNP = who owns the production (the country's citizens, wherever they are)
Audi is a German company. Cars made at Audi's plant in Belgium count in:
→ Belgian GDP (production happens in Belgium)
→ German GNP (Audi is a German company)

Formula: GNP = GDP + residents' overseas income − foreign residents' income earned here
Nominal GDP vs Real GDP

If GDP rises year-on-year, two things could be happening:

  • Production actually increased (good)
  • Just prices rose (inflation — production unchanged)
Nominal (money) GDP: measured at current prices. Affected by inflation.

Real GDP: measured using a BASE YEAR's prices. Strips out inflation. Shows true changes in production.
Real GDP (year B) = Money GDP (year B) × [Price Index Year A ÷ Price Index Year B]
Growth = (Real GDP Year B − Real GDP Year A) ÷ Real GDP Year A
Real GDP per capita = Real GDP ÷ Population
Used to measure standard of living
Three ways to measure national income (all give the same answer)
National output = National expenditure = National income

a) Output (value-added) approach

Sum the value added at each stage of production.

The bread example:
a) Farmer grows wheat, sells for $1 → value added = $1
b) Miller buys wheat for $1, sells flour for $2 → value added = $1
c) Baker buys flour for $2, sells bread for $5 → value added = $3
d) Grocer buys bread for $5, sells for $9 → value added = $4

Total: $1 + $1 + $3 + $4 = $9 = final price of the bread

b) Expenditure approach

GDP = C + I + G + (X − M) = AD

C = consumption, I = investment, G = government spending, X−M = net exports.

c) Income approach

Sum all income earned: wages + rent + interest + profit (taxes and depreciation excluded).

Why GDP is a flawed measure of welfare

Things GDP misses

  • Black market / underground economy
  • Household production (cooking, DIY, growing vegetables)
  • Voluntary unpaid work
  • Subsistence farming in developing countries
  • Illegal activities
  • Second-hand sales between individuals

Things GDP ignores

  • Income distribution (just an average — your notes give the Norway vs Kuwait example: both have similar real GDP per head, but very different inequality levels)
  • What is produced (guns vs education? Capital vs consumer? Merit vs demerit?)
  • Environmental damage
  • Sustainability (over-exploiting nature today hurts tomorrow)
  • Working conditions
  • Health and education indicators

Comparing countries

  • Different price levels → use PPP (Purchasing Power Parity)
  • Different currencies → convert to a common one (USD)
  • Different accounting standards
  • Corruption / unreliable data
GDP is necessary but not sufficient to measure development. Other indicators (HDI, GDI) add literacy, life expectancy, gender equality.

E3. Aggregate Demand (AD)

Aggregate demand = total spending on all goods and services by all economic agents in an economy, at a given price level, over a given period.
AD = C + I + G + (X − M)
The four components explained

C — Consumption (by households)

  • Durable goods (used over time): computers, cars, fridges
  • Non-durable goods (used quickly): Coca-Cola, toothpaste
  • Services (non-tangible): insurance, plumbing

I — Investment (by firms)

  • Replacement investment: replacing old capital
  • Induced investment: NEW capital that increases productive capacity

G — Government spending

  • Capital spending: infrastructure, schools, hospitals
  • Current spending: day-to-day running (medicines, electricity)
G does NOT include transfer payments (pensions, unemployment benefits) — those just move money around without paying for production.

X − M — Net exports

Imports must be subtracted because C, I and G already include imported goods. Imports are part of someone else's GDP, not ours.

What shifts the AD curve?

Change in price level → MOVE along the curve.
Change in any component (C, I, G, X−M) → SHIFT the curve.

What changes C (consumption)?

  • Disposable income, consumer confidence, interest rates, taxes
  • Inflation (double effect: buy now / money loses value)
  • Credit availability, household composition

What changes I (investment)?

  • Interest rates, national income, savings, profits
  • Political/economic stability, business confidence

What changes G (government spending)?

  • Fiscal policy: spending and taxation rules (expansionary or contractionary)
  • Monetary policy: central bank controls money supply and interest rates

What changes X − M?

  • Foreign incomes, exchange rates, trade policies, inflation differences
  • Imports rise when our national income rises
Consumption and savings — formulas
Yd = C + S
Disposable income = Consumption + Savings

Average propensities

  • APC = C ÷ Yd (e.g. APC = 0.6 → spends 60% of income)
  • APS = S ÷ Yd
  • APC + APS = 1 (always)

Marginal propensities

  • MPC = ΔC ÷ ΔYd (if MPC = 0.8 and income rises €1000, C rises €800)
  • MPS = ΔS ÷ ΔYd
  • Low-income people have HIGHER MPC than wealthy people

Keynesian consumption function

C = C₀ + MPC × Yd
C₀ = minimum consumption (even at zero income, people need to consume)
S = −C₀ + MPS × Yd

E4. The Multiplier Effect

The big idea: When the government injects €100m into the economy, the FINAL increase in national income is BIGGER than €100m. Why? Because that money becomes someone else's income, which they spend, which becomes someone else's income, and on and on. Each round multiplies the original injection.

Example: Government spends €100m on a school.

  • Architects, engineers, builders receive the €100m
  • Steel suppliers, glass makers get paid
  • Those workers earn wages — they spend on food, clothes, holidays
  • Shopkeepers earn — they spend again
  • Continues, but each round is smaller (some money "leaks" out)

What stops the multiplier?

At each round, money leaks out via:

  • MPS — marginal propensity to save
  • MPT — marginal propensity to tax
  • MPM — marginal propensity to import
k = 1 ÷ (1 − MPC) = 1 ÷ (MPS + MPT + MPM)
ΔY = k × Δ(injection)
  • Higher MPC → higher multiplier (money keeps circulating)
  • Higher leakages → lower multiplier (money escapes flow)
Worked example:
MPC = 0.8. Government spends an extra €100m.

Step 1: k = 1 ÷ (1 − 0.8) = 1 ÷ 0.2 = 5
Step 2: ΔY = 5 × €100m = €500m

The €100m injection raises national income by €500m.

The Business (Trade) Cycle

Real economies don't grow steadily — they go through cycles. Four stages:

StageWhat happens
ExpansionGDP growing, employment rising, confidence high
PeakTop of cycle — full capacity, risk of inflation
Contraction / recessionGDP falling, unemployment rising (officially: two consecutive quarters of negative growth)
TroughBottom — high unemployment, low confidence — but recovery begins

Two output-gap ideas:

  • Positive output gap: producing ABOVE long-term trend → inflation
  • Negative output gap: producing BELOW trend → unemployment, spare capacity
